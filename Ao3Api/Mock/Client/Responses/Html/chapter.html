Chapter Text

It’d been weeks since Dorian joined the Inquisition, and he hadn’t been this homesick in a long time.

Skyhold was an acceptable place to call home, for now, as he prepared to fight Corypheus and his army alongside quite the hodgepodge of people.

The Inquisitor, or Herald of Andraste for the devout, was a surprisingly conscientious leader and a sophisticated strategist. His rise from insignificance to become the newest and most formidable power in all of Thedas was stuff legends were made of.

Dorian knew better than to pin his quick ascension solely on the magical mark he bore in the palm of his hand. The man was a fine leader and Dorian trusted his decisions implicitly.

Which was not to say that they were on friendly terms. Dorian had come to a rueful acceptance that, from all indications, the Inquisitor didn’t care for his company. After their initial and very hectic first meetings, Trevelyan had sought him out for questioning.

He'd wanted to know about Alexius and his role in Corypheus' grand plans. He asked to understand the basic architecture of time magic. And, like most people Dorian had met outside of Tevinter, the Inquisitor was curious to know what life was like in the Imperium.

Dorian remembered gesticulating emphatically, answering all his queries with reasonable honesty, when Trevelyan’s eyes widened at him - in alarm? - and swallowed hard enough to make his throat move. Though he swiftly schooled his features back into an impassive mask, it was not quite quick enough to escape Dorian's notice.

Was it something he'd said? It was perhaps foolish of him to try to explain Tevinters' stance on slavery. Was it admitting involvement in developing dangerous time-bending magic? Or did his sympathy toward his former mentor appear a warning sign that he was not to be trusted?

Whatever it was that had offended the Inquisitor’s delicate sensibilities, it mattered little in the grand scheme of things. Dorian did not make the trek across the continent to make friends, anyway.

Still, he had hoped the leader of the Inquisition didn't find his presence completely off-putting, too 'Tevinter-y' and all, even if everyone else did. But from the way the man had been actively avoiding him, and only ever invited him to take part in the briefest of missions...

Well, Dorian was many things, but he wasn’t dense. He could tell when his presence was unwanted.

Besides, Dorian thought with an indignant sniff, the feeling was entirely mutual.

There was something not quite right about the Inquisitor that he couldn’t put his finger on. The man's dour demeanour aside, he always had a hint of sourness that followed him wherever he went, rather like fish that had been left out for too long in the sun.

And Dorian knew he wasn’t the only one who noticed. He'd caught Vivienne angle her head away discreetly once, a thin smile pinched on her face, while Trevelyan spoke with her about recovering some lost tomes in the Hinterlands.

Dorian wrinkled his nose at the memory. It must be the poor hygiene. Southerners seemed to hold dear this practice of not bathing - evidently, even Free Matcher nobles like Trevelyan found it perfectly natural to smell like barn animals.

He inhaled a long breath, clearing the residual olfactory reminder with the scent of parchment and melting wax. East facing, his nook in the library was warmed by the rays of the afternoon sun. Outside, the subject of his aggravating reflections sparred with the Iron Bull. Through the small window, Dorian watched as the two warriors circled one another in the training pit, kicking up sand and dust as theirs metal blades clashed over and over again.

Trevelyan was a tall man - standing at least several inches over six feet, he commanded a powerful presence and was a truly terrifying force on the battlefield. But even from a distance, Dorian could tell it was strenuous exercise for a human to take on a Qunari as big as the Bull. Trevelyan's skills were certainly being put to the test.

They must have been training for hours by now. The Inquisitor's pale shirt clung to his body in damp patches, outlining his broad shoulders and narrow waist. If Dorian squinted hard enough, he could almost see through the thin tunic as the fabric slowly turned translucent to reveal strong muscles, strained and bulging as the Inquisitor spun his greatsword with both hands.

Dorian committed all those details to memory, for later. After all, he didn’t have to like the man to appreciate his more palatable attributes.

 

* * *

 

When Dorian was finally asked to go on a mission with the Inquisitor, after being sidelined for weeks, he didn’t think it would mean herding cattle in the Hinterlands.

“I know it is imperative that we win the good will of the local populace,” he began as they followed the trail up a grassy hill, “and not that I’m not grateful to be out of Skyhold every now and again, but is it absolutely necessary that we degrade ourselves to doing farmers' work?”

The Iron Bull frowned, shifting the weight of his brand new axe on his shoulder. “There is nothing degrading about growing your own food.”

“You know what I mean,” Dorian scoffed. “Look at us here! You are arguably our strongest warrior-”

“Arguably?”

Dorian rolled his eyes. “And Solas here is clearly our second best mage.” He snuck a glance at the elf and was slightly annoyed at the lack of reaction. “Then, we have the Inquisitor, the bearer of the powerful mark of the Fade, and lastly, of course, yours truly – only one of the most gifted mages in recent Tevinter history. My question is: Do they really need the best the Inquisition has to offer to find a druffalo named Druffy?”

The Iron Bull grunted. “Less talking, more walking, Vint.”

Trevelyan said nothing, continued his quiet march forward with his eyes trained on the trek. But from the side, Dorian could see he had a slight smile on his lips, and it warmed him to think that at least the Herald of bloody Andraste found him amusing.  

Half-hour later, Dorian realised rather belatedly why the Inquisitor had assembled such a robust party to search for the blasted animal.

The fade rift that nestled in the Forannan Ravine was the most challenging one they had encountered yet, spewing unending hoards of demons that simply refused to die. Hooded despair demons twirled around them tauntingly, shooting deadly ice beams at devastating speeds. Long-limbed terror demons trampled over them, crooked talons leaving deep marks on their armours as they ducked and dodged the worst of the attacks.

After what felt like hours, with everyone pushed to their limits – Iron Bull even passed out at one point, though he’d never admit to it – they vanquished the last of those wretched things and safely closed the rift.

Completely drained and sopped in sticky demon blood, Dorian barely caught the healing potion Trevelyan tossed in his direction. He downed it in one draught, feeling a little more renewed when the potion went to work closing his wounds and dulling the aches to a quiet hum.

“What about you?” Dorian asked when Trevelyan turned to walk away, one hand touching gingerly a bleeding gash on his side.

“That was the last one," he said simply. “I only have to be up and standing until the rift closes anyway.”

Something in his words stirred and unsettled Dorian. He stared at the man’s retreating back, the empty vial heavy in his hand.

 

* * *

“Where is the Inquisitor?” Dorian asked out loud to no one in particular.

They had successfully reunited Druffy with its worried owner, thankfully encountering no further demon attacks. If only the damned beast didn’t take an age to follow them across the ravine, it wouldn’t have been nightfall already by the time they returned to camp.

Trevelyan had disappeared off somewhere without saying a word. Dorian wasn’t worried, not exactly, but the wound did look a little bit nasty.

“Bull?”

The Qunari shrugged, too busy scrubbing the muck off of his axe with a wet rag. “Try the river. He had blood all over him. Maybe he went to clean up.”

The Inquisitor bathing? With actual water? And soap, too, one would hope. Dorian couldn’t fathom missing out on such a monumental event that would provide a year's worth of needling material. And certainly not one as pleasing to the eye.

So Dorian made his way back to the ravine where, only mere hours ago, was swarming with demons. Now, with the rift closed, the eerie green glow of the river gave way to the pale yellow of moonlight, gentle ripples glimmering invitingly.

The moons were so much larger and brighter than what he was used to back in Tevinter. Out here in the arse end of Ferelden, there were no brightly-lit towers, no ever-burning magefires, no slaves carrying torches and lanterns to light the way. Only him and the serene silver moons.

A splash in the river pulled him back to reality as a lone figure emerged from underwater. Dorian watched as Trevelyan shook the water from his hair and slicked back wet tendrils with a hand. He was gloriously shirtless, wearing only a pair of thin braies that hung low on his hips, and the well-worn leather bracers Dorian had never seen him without. His bronze skin glistened as water rivulets cascaded southward, finding paths down the landscape of that massive chest and the deep ridges of his stomach.

Trevelyan was breathtakingly beautiful. And smelly.

The odd, acidic scent had returned, stronger than it'd been before. The stench hung heavy in the air, and Dorian was glad to have it break the spell. He absolutely did not want to feel anything for the man beyond a healthy appreciation for muscled male form.

He cleared his throat before speaking. “Forgive me for overstepping, my dear Inquisitor, but have you ever considered using herbal soaks for that stink? Or at least give your armpits an extra scrub?”

Trevelyan looked positively startled at his voice, and Dorian couldn’t quite hide the smirk at having snuck up on a seasoned warrior.

Before he could begin his rant about how he could have easily been an assassin looking to claim the Herald's head, he saw the Inquisitor's hands pressed to his injured side, the anchor's faint radiance slowly dissipating under his palms.

“Dorian.” Trevelyan's eyes riveted on him. “What are you doing here?”

“What's going on over there? You’re not trying to heal your wound with the mark, are you?” A beat later, curiosity won over. “Wait, can it?”

“No, I wasn’t. And no, it can't," Trevelyan said a little curtly. “Did you need something?”

“But there was a strange glow-”

“The mark sometimes has a mind of its own. You needn’t be troubled by it.” Trevelyan held up his left hand, the anchor now dimmed to flicker of green. “We both had a long day, best if we return to camp quickly. I’ll be right behind you.”

Dorian knew that was a polite way of telling him to shut it and leave. If only the man didn't speak with him with the most guarded countenance, with the most measured voice. If only they had the rapport so he could prod and pry until Trevelyan spilled all his secrets. Among other things, his mind added rather unhelpfully. 

He allowed himself one more glance at the Inquisitor’s delightful display of flesh, and bowed courteously before turning on his heels to walk back to camp.
